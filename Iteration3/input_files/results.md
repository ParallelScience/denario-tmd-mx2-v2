## 1. Data Preprocessing and Target Definition

The initial phase of the study involved preparing the dataset of 202 binary MX₂ transition-metal dichalcogenides (TMDs) for machine learning applications. A critical electronic descriptor, the density of states at the Fermi level (`dos_at_fermi`), was missing for 16 materials. Because the density of states is a fundamental proxy for electronic stability and potential structural distortions, discarding these samples would have resulted in a significant loss of chemical information. To preserve the dataset's size and integrity, a k-nearest neighbors (KNN) imputation strategy ($k=5$) was employed using the standardized continuous feature space. The imputed values exhibited a mean of 4.2795 states/eV and a median of 3.1713 states/eV, which aligned well with the distribution of the 186 known values (mean: 3.8266 states/eV, median: 2.3852 states/eV). The standard deviation of the imputed set (3.8200) was slightly lower than that of the known set (4.7638), reflecting the expected variance reduction typical of KNN imputation. A binary indicator feature, `is_dos_missing`, was retained to allow the model to account for any systematic uncertainty introduced by the imputation process, ensuring that the predictive algorithm could weight these specific samples appropriately.

The primary objective of this study was to classify metastable TMDs based on their mechanical robustness, specifically targeting materials with high shear moduli ($G_{vrh}$). Rather than treating $G_{vrh}$ as a continuous regression target—which is often fraught with high error margins in small datasets and highly sensitive to DFT calculation parameters—we framed the problem as a binary classification task. The baseline threshold for mechanical robustness was defined using the median $G_{vrh}$ of the thermodynamically stable population within the 90-sample subset that possessed computed elastic constants. This median value was determined to be 16.102 GPa. Applying this threshold yielded an initial class distribution of 35 "robust" (Class 1) and 55 "non-robust" (Class 0) materials.

To ensure the robustness of this definition and to understand how the classification boundary impacts the selection of candidates, a rigorous sensitivity analysis was conducted by varying the classification threshold between the 60th and 80th percentiles of the stable population's $G_{vrh}$. As the threshold increased from 17.9352 GPa (60th percentile) to 29.1938 GPa (80th percentile), the number of materials classified as robust naturally decreased from 26 to 14. By intersecting the robust candidates across all tested thresholds, we identified a "high-confidence" set of 14 materials (including mp-1008682, mp-1018804, mp-1405065, mp-1522, mp-19880, mp-2070, mp-20862, mp-20901, mp-21321, mp-22309, mp-226, mp-760, mp-9027, and mp-9945) that consistently exhibited exceptional mechanical stability regardless of the stringency of the threshold. This high-confidence set serves as a benchmark for the upper echelon of mechanical performance in TMDs, representing structural motifs that are inherently resistant to shear deformation.

## 2. Model Performance and Cross-Validation

To predict the mechanical robustness of TMDs, a Random Forest classifier was trained on the 90-sample subset with known elastic properties. Given the chemical diversity of the dataset, which spans 13 transition metals across different groups and periods, standard k-fold cross-validation could lead to overly optimistic performance estimates due to data leakage between structurally and chemically similar compounds. To mitigate this and rigorously test the model's extrapolative capabilities, a Leave-One-Cluster-Out (LOCO) cross-validation strategy was implemented, grouping the data by the transition metal (`metal`). This approach ensures that the model is evaluated on its ability to generalize to entirely unseen chemical families based purely on their electronic and structural descriptors.

The Random Forest model, configured with 100 estimators and balanced class weights to handle the slight class imbalance, demonstrated strong predictive capabilities. The macro-averaged metrics across the held-out metal groups were highly encouraging: an Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve (AUPRC) of 0.9028, an Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic curve (AUROC) of 0.9380, and an F1 score of 0.6812.

Performance varied significantly depending on the specific transition metal held out during validation, highlighting the complex interplay between chemistry and mechanics. For several metal groups, including Cobalt (Co), Chromium (Cr), Manganese (Mn), Titanium (Ti), and Vanadium (V), the model achieved perfect AUPRC and AUROC scores of 1.0. This indicates that the electronic and structural descriptors learned from other metals perfectly generalized to these families. Conversely, the model struggled with certain groups, notably Niobium (Nb) and Molybdenum (Mo), which yielded lower AUPRC scores of 0.7333 and 0.7556, respectively. Niobium and Molybdenum are Group 5 and 6 metals that frequently form 2H and 1T phases known for complex charge-density-wave (CDW) transitions. The mechanical softening associated with these CDWs is highly sensitive to subtle Fermi surface nesting features, which may not be fully captured by a simple scalar like `dos_at_fermi`. The F1 scores for Zirconium (Zr) and Hafnium (Hf) were 0.0, though it is important to note that these groups contained very few samples (3 each), making metric calculation highly sensitive to single misclassifications. For Iron (Fe) and Nickel (Ni), AUPRC and AUROC could not be computed due to the lack of class diversity in the test folds, but the model achieved perfect F1 scores of 1.0 for both, correctly predicting the majority class. Overall, the high macro-averaged AUPRC confirms that the model successfully captured the underlying physical rules governing mechanical robustness across diverse TMD chemistries.

## 3. Feature Ablation and the Role of Spin-Orbit Coupling

A central hypothesis of this research was that spin-dependent structural distortions, driven by spin-orbit coupling (SOC) and high electronic density of states, suppress mechanical stability. Heavy transition metals with strong SOC often exhibit complex band structures where orbital degeneracy is lifted, potentially leading to Jahn-Teller-like distortions that soften the crystal lattice. To test this hypothesis quantitatively, a feature ablation study was conducted to isolate the contributions of the SOC proxy (`M_soc_proxy`) and the density of states at the Fermi level (`dos_at_fermi`).

Four model configurations were evaluated using the LOCO cross-validation scheme: the Full Set, a model without `M_soc_proxy`, a model without `dos_at_fermi`, and a model excluding both features. The Full Set achieved a macro-averaged AUPRC of 0.9028. Removing `M_soc_proxy` resulted in a slight decrease in performance, yielding an AUPRC of 0.8906. Interestingly, removing `dos_at_fermi` actually improved the macro-averaged AUPRC to 0.9236, and removing both features resulted in an AUPRC of 0.9147.

To determine if the performance drop associated with the removal of `M_soc_proxy` was statistically meaningful, a paired t-test was performed on the fold-wise AUPRC scores. The mean AUPRC difference between the Full Set and the "No M_soc_proxy" configuration was 0.0122, with a p-value of 0.6378. Because the p-value significantly exceeds the standard alpha level of 0.05, we conclude that the performance drop is statistically negligible. Consequently, the hypothesis that spin-orbit coupling is a primary, independent driver of mechanical softening in this specific modeling framework is rejected. The model appears to capture the necessary variance related to mechanical stability through other correlated electronic and structural descriptors (such as atomic mass, electronegativity differences, or specific space group symmetries), rendering the explicit inclusion of `M_soc_proxy` redundant for this specific classification task. The slight performance increase upon removing `dos_at_fermi` suggests that while the density of states is physically relevant, its current representation as a single scalar, or the noise introduced by the imputation process, might slightly hinder the Random Forest's generalization across certain metal groups prone to complex electronic instabilities.

## 4. Out-of-Distribution (OOD) Detection

Before deploying the trained model to screen the 112 metastable candidates, it was imperative to ensure that these candidates fell within the chemical and structural domain of applicability defined by the training set. Machine learning models, particularly tree-based ensembles, are notoriously poor at extrapolating beyond their training distribution. An Out-of-Distribution (OOD) detection mechanism was implemented using the k-nearest neighbors (KNN) distance in the standardized continuous feature space. The internal distance distribution of the stable training set was computed, and the 95th percentile of this distribution—calculated to be 17.8542—was established as the strict OOD threshold.

Applying this threshold to the metastable candidates identified four materials as "chemically exotic" or OOD: mp-680184 (TaSe₂; distance = 28.6348), mp-1024087 (Zr₆₃S₁₂₈; distance = 36.2066), mp-1096929 (Zr₆₄S₁₂₇; distance = 38.2034), and mp-1094089 (NiS₂; distance = 19.9352). The two Zirconium-sulfur compounds exhibit highly unusual stoichiometries and massive unit cells (with over 190 sites), clearly separating them from the standard MX₂ phases that dominate the dataset. The TaSe₂ and NiS₂ polymorphs flagged as OOD likely possess extreme structural distortions, unusual lattice parameters, or anomalous electronic properties not represented in the stable training data.

To visualize the feature space and the separation of these OOD candidates, a t-SNE (t-Distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding) projection was generated. The resulting 2D map effectively clustered the materials, with the stable training samples forming a cohesive, dense core. The metastable candidates predicted to be robust ("Metastable-Robust") largely overlapped with this stable core, indicating that their electronic and structural profiles are highly analogous to known stable, robust materials. In contrast, the four "Metastable-OOD" candidates were visibly isolated on the periphery of the projection, validating the KNN-based distance metric and confirming that predictions for these materials should be treated with high skepticism.

## 5. Model Interpretation and Electronic Signatures of Robustness

To unpack the "black box" of the Random Forest model and understand the physical drivers of mechanical robustness, SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) were employed. The SHAP summary analysis revealed that structural parameters such as unit cell volume, lattice parameters, and space group classifications were dominant predictors. However, electronic descriptors, particularly `d_band_filling` and `dos_at_fermi`, played crucial roles in modulating the model's decisions, confirming the study's premise that electronic phase-space mapping is viable for predicting mechanical properties.

A deep dive into the interaction effects between `d_band_filling` and `dos_at_fermi` was conducted, with a specific focus on Nickel-based TMDs. Nickel compounds are of particular interest due to their diverse magnetic and electronic behaviors, often exhibiting complex phase diagrams and strong electron correlation effects. The SHAP interaction values for the Ni-based subset (where the standardized `d_band_filling` is consistently high, at a value of 4.0) revealed a nuanced relationship.

Among the known stable Ni-based TMDs, materials like mp-1180046 and mp-2578 are classified as robust. These materials exhibit relatively low or negative standardized `dos_at_fermi` values (-0.4786 and -0.1561, respectively). In contrast, metastable Ni-based candidates exhibit a wide spread of `dos_at_fermi` values, ranging from -0.4786 to 1.1854. The SHAP interaction plot demonstrated that for Ni-based TMDs, a lower density of states at the Fermi level correlates positively with mechanical robustness. This observation aligns with the physical intuition that a high density of states at the Fermi level often leads to electronic instabilities—such as Jahn-Teller distortions, charge density waves, or magnetic phase transitions—that drive structural phase transitions and concomitant mechanical softening. By minimizing the DOS at the Fermi level, the crystal lattice avoids these symmetry-breaking distortions, thereby maintaining a higher shear modulus. The model successfully learned this non-linear, physics-based interaction, utilizing it to differentiate between robust and non-robust polymorphs within the same chemical family.

## 6. Prioritization of Metastable Candidates for Experimental Synthesis

The ultimate goal of this framework is to accelerate materials discovery by providing experimentalists with a prioritized list of metastable TMDs that are highly likely to be mechanically robust. The trained Random Forest model was applied to the 112 metastable candidates to predict their probability of belonging to the robust class.

To ensure the highest quality of recommendations, a stringent filtering process was applied. First, the four candidates flagged as OOD were excluded to prevent extrapolation errors and ensure that the candidates are synthetically reasonable within the known TMD phase space. Second, a high-confidence probability threshold of 0.75 was enforced. Out of the initial 112 candidates, 28 materials successfully passed these criteria, representing the most promising targets for future synthesis.

The top of the prioritized list is heavily dominated by specific chemical families, notably sulfides and selenides of Iron, Cobalt, and Nickel. The highest-ranked candidate is a polymorph of NiS₂ (mp-849059) with a predicted probability of 1.0. This is followed closely by FeSe₂ (mp-1103177, probability 0.98), NiSe₂ (mp-1071078, probability 0.98), CoS₂ (mp-850049, probability 0.97), and another NiS₂ polymorph (mp-850068, probability 0.97).

The prevalence of Fe, Co, and Ni compounds in the top rankings is physically significant. These late transition metals, characterized by higher d-band fillings, often form pyrite or marcasite-type structures (frequently classified under 'cubic' or 'other' in the standard 2H/1T TMD nomenclature) which are known for their dense atomic packing, strong covalent bonding networks, and three-dimensional structural rigidity compared to the van der Waals bonded 2H or 1T phases. The model has effectively identified that within the metastable phase space, these specific electronic configurations and structural motifs are highly conducive to mechanical rigidity.

Furthermore, the top candidates generally exhibit moderate to low KNN distances (e.g., ranging from 3.7 to 7.2), indicating that while they are metastable, their feature profiles are well-represented by the stable training data, lending high confidence to the model's predictions. For instance, the top-ranked NiS₂ (mp-849059) has a KNN distance of 4.2827, placing it comfortably within the known distribution. Other notable candidates include MnS₂ (mp-1455, probability 0.93), TiS₂ (mp-1102845, probability 0.80), and CrS₂ (mp-1442954, probability 0.79), showcasing that while late transition metals dominate, robust metastable phases can be found across the transition metal series.

## Conclusion and Implications for Experimental Synthesis

This study successfully developed a machine learning framework to map the electronic phase space of mechanical robustness in binary MX₂ TMDs. By defining a stability boundary based on the median shear modulus of stable phases, we trained a Random Forest classifier that demonstrated excellent generalization across diverse transition metal chemistries, achieving a Macro-Avg AUPRC of 0.9028.

Crucially, the feature ablation study revealed that while spin-orbit coupling (`M_soc_proxy`) was hypothesized to be a primary driver of mechanical softening, its explicit inclusion was statistically negligible for the model's predictive power. Instead, the model relied on complex interactions between structural parameters and electronic descriptors like `d_band_filling` and `dos_at_fermi`. The SHAP analysis confirmed that for specific families, such as Ni-based TMDs, a lower density of states at the Fermi level acts as a strong indicator of mechanical robustness, likely by preventing electronic instabilities that lead to structural softening.

The integration of an Out-of-Distribution detection mechanism ensured that the final predictions were constrained to the model's domain of applicability, successfully filtering out chemically exotic phases. The final output is a highly curated list of 28 metastable TMDs, prioritized by their probability of exhibiting high shear moduli. The prominence of Fe, Co, and Ni sulfides and selenides at the top of this list provides clear, actionable targets for experimentalists. Synthesizing these specific polymorphs could yield new materials that combine the unique electronic and magnetic properties of metastable TMDs with the mechanical durability required for integration into next-generation flexible electronics, protective coatings, and advanced nanodevices. The methodology presented here—combining robust target definition, rigorous cross-validation, physical feature ablation, and OOD detection—provides a comprehensive blueprint for the data-driven discovery of mechanically viable metastable materials.
