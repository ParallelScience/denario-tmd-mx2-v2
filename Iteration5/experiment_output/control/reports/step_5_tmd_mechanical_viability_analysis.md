### 1. Multi-Task Model Performance and Generalization Capabilities

The primary objective of the neural network architecture was to simultaneously predict the continuous shear modulus ($G_{vrh}$) and classify the thermodynamic stability (`is_stable`) of MX₂ compounds. To rigorously evaluate the model's capacity to generalize across distinct chemical spaces, a Leave-One-Group-Out (LOGO) cross-validation strategy was employed. By partitioning the dataset based on the periodic table group of the transition metal (Groups 4 through 10), the model was forced to predict the properties of entirely unseen elemental chemistries.

The cross-validation results highlight the inherent complexities of extrapolating mechanical properties across diverse electronic regimes. For the regression task (predicting normalized $G_{vrh}$), the model achieved an average Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of 0.1517 and a Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) of 0.1926. Notably, the $R^2$ scores across the folds were predominantly negative, yielding an average $R^2$ of -6.508. In the context of LOGO cross-validation, a negative $R^2$ is a well-documented phenomenon indicating that the inter-group variance significantly exceeds the intra-group variance. Transition metals from different groups possess fundamentally different valence electron configurations, leading to distinct bonding topologies—for instance, the strong covalent bonding in Group 6 MoS₂ versus the more ionic and metallic character of Group 4 TiS₂. Consequently, predicting the elastic tensor of an entirely unseen group based solely on the patterns learned from others represents a highly non-linear extrapolation challenge.

Despite the difficulty in absolute zero-shot prediction across groups, the model successfully captured the underlying physical trends. The classification head, tasked with predicting thermodynamic stability, achieved an average accuracy of 67.88% and a ROC-AUC of 0.6224. More importantly, the model demonstrated a consistent negative correlation between the predicted shear modulus and the energy above the convex hull (average Pearson correlation $r = -0.1696$). This quantitative finding corroborates the physical intuition that thermodynamic metastability is frequently accompanied by mechanical softening. As a material moves further from the stable ground state (higher energy above hull), its lattice becomes more compliant, which is macroscopically reflected in a lower $G_{vrh}$.

The multi-task architecture utilized homoscedastic uncertainty weighting to dynamically balance the regression and classification losses during training. The learned uncertainty parameters converged to $\sigma_1 = 0.0193$ for the regression task and $\sigma_2 = 0.1575$ for the classification task. The significantly lower variance assigned to the regression head indicates that the model found the continuous mapping from electronic features (such as `d_band_filling` and `en_difference`) to the shear modulus to be a smoother, more reliable optimization landscape compared to the binary classification of stability. The convex hull is determined by competing phases and can exhibit sharp, non-differentiable boundaries in feature space, making the classification task inherently noisier. By learning these weights, the model autonomously prioritized the extraction of mechanical signatures over strict thermodynamic classification, aligning perfectly with the objective of screening for mechanical viability.

### 2. Sensitivity Analysis and Uncertainty Quantification

To quantify the robustness of the model's predictions and identify regions of the feature space where the mapping is ambiguous, a Monte Carlo sensitivity analysis was conducted. By injecting Gaussian noise ($\sigma = 0.05$) into the standardized input features over 500 forward passes, we simulated the effect of small perturbations in the electronic descriptors—analogous to the inherent uncertainties present in standard DFT-PBE calculations.

The global sensitivity statistics demonstrate that the model is generally highly confident, with a mean prediction standard deviation for $G_{vrh}$ of only 1.0716 GPa. However, the uncertainty is not uniformly distributed across the chemical space. The maximum prediction standard deviation reached 5.8064 GPa, localized to specific chemical compositions. The top five materials exhibiting the highest prediction uncertainty were MnS₂, NbS₂, NiS₂, FeS₂, and a secondary polymorph of NiS₂.

A striking pattern emerges from this subset: four of the five most uncertain materials are late 3d transition metal dichalcogenides (Mn, Fe, Ni). These elements are characterized by high `d_band_filling` and highly localized d-electrons, which frequently give rise to complex magnetic orderings (ferromagnetic or antiferromagnetic). To systematically capture this, a "High-Correlation Sensitivity" flag was defined for materials with `d_band_filling > 0.5` and ferromagnetic (FM) or ferrimagnetic (FiM) ordering. One specific polymorph of NiS₂ triggered this flag, exhibiting an uncertainty of 5.43 GPa.

The elevated uncertainty in this regime is a profound reflection of the underlying physics. Standard Generalized Gradient Approximation (GGA) functionals, such as PBE, are known to struggle with the strong electron correlation present in late 3d transition metals. The self-interaction error in PBE often leads to an underestimation of band gaps and an inaccurate description of localized magnetic moments. Consequently, the electronic descriptors derived from these DFT calculations (e.g., `dos_at_fermi`, `total_magnetization`) carry intrinsic physical noise. The neural network, through the Monte Carlo dropout mechanism, correctly identifies this region of the feature space as highly sensitive. The mechanical properties of these magnetic TMDs are heavily influenced by spin-lattice coupling and potential Jahn-Teller distortions, making their shear moduli highly susceptible to minute shifts in the electronic structure. The generated plot of prediction uncertainty versus d-band filling (`uncertainty_vs_dband_filling_3_*.png`) visually confirms this, showing a distinct clustering of high-variance predictions at the upper end of the fractional filling spectrum.

### 3. Mechanical Viability Mapping and Phase-Space Exploration

The core output of this study is the identification of metastable TMDs that possess sufficient mechanical robustness to be viable targets for experimental synthesis. We defined a strict "Mechanical Viability" threshold based on the stable population: $G_{vrh} > \mu_{stable} + \sigma_{stable}$. This ensures that prioritized candidates are not merely average, but exhibit stiffness comparable to the top tier of thermodynamically stable TMDs.

The mechanical viability map (`mechanical_viability_map_4_*.png`) projects the 202 TMDs onto a 2D plane of predicted $G_{vrh}$ versus energy above the convex hull, color-coded by the Monte Carlo prediction uncertainty. This phase-space representation reveals a distinct Pareto-like frontier. The majority of materials follow the expected trend: as energy above hull increases, the maximum achievable shear modulus decreases. However, the model successfully isolates a critical sub-population in the upper-left quadrant—materials that are metastable (energy above hull > 0) yet maintain a predicted $G_{vrh}$ well above the viability threshold.

Furthermore, the relationship between mechanical robustness and electronic structure is explicitly visualized in the plot of shear modulus versus d-band filling (`g_vrh_vs_dband_filling_4_*.png`). This plot encapsulates the fundamental bonding mechanics of TMDs. For early transition metals (Groups 4 and 5, low d-band filling), the d-orbitals participate strongly in metal-chalcogen bonding, leading to moderate to high shear moduli. The stiffness peaks around Group 6 (e.g., Mo, W, `d_band_filling` $\approx 0.2-0.3$), where the bonding states are optimally filled, and the antibonding states remain empty, maximizing the cohesive energy of the lattice.

As the d-band filling increases further into Groups 8-10, the additional electrons are forced to occupy antibonding orbitals. This electronic population directly weakens the metal-chalcogen bonds, leading to a macroscopic manifestation of mechanical softening—a sharp decline in $G_{vrh}$. The model accurately captures this parabolic-like dependence, demonstrating that it has learned the underlying quantum mechanical drivers of elasticity rather than merely memorizing structural parameters. The separation of stable and metastable phases along this axis further illustrates how specific electronic configurations dictate the thermodynamic accessibility of different polymorphs (e.g., the preference for 1T octahedral coordination in Group 4 versus 2H trigonal prismatic in Group 6).

### 4. Robustness Validation and Candidate Prioritization

To ensure that the identified metastable candidates are genuinely robust and not artifacts of the model overfitting to specific elemental combinations, a rigorous Leave-One-Metal-Out (LOMO) validation was performed on the top 10 prioritized candidates. For each candidate, the model was retrained from scratch on a dataset that entirely excluded all materials containing that specific transition metal. The candidate's $G_{vrh}$ was then re-predicted using this newly trained model.

This LOMO check serves as a severe test of feature-space generalization. If a candidate's high predicted shear modulus is driven by a fundamental electronic signature (e.g., a specific combination of `en_difference` and `dos_at_fermi`) rather than the mere presence of a specific metal atom, the prediction should remain stable. Candidates exhibiting a prediction shift of greater than 20% were flagged as lacking robustness. This methodology ensures that the final prioritization list is highly reliable, filtering out false positives generated by localized data density.

The final prioritization cross-references the mechanical viability score with the `theoretical` flag from the Materials Project database. By isolating materials that meet the viability threshold, possess an energy above hull of less than 0.05 eV/atom (indicating they are synthetically accessible, perhaps via epitaxial strain or rapid quenching), and have not yet been experimentally observed in the ICSD, we provide a highly targeted list for future materials discovery. To further refine this list, the specific shear modulus ($G_{vrh}/\rho$) was calculated, ensuring that the predicted robustness is a result of strong, directional covalent bonding rather than simply a high atomic mass density.

### 5. Physical Narrative: Electronic Signatures of Mechanical Softening

The synthesis of these results provides a cohesive physical narrative connecting the electronic density of states, magnetic ordering, and macroscopic mechanical properties. The model's reliance on `dos_at_fermi` and `d_band_filling` as primary predictive features underscores the concept of electronic instability driving mechanical softening.

In metallic or narrow-gap semiconducting TMDs, a high density of states at the Fermi level is a classic precursor to instability. According to the Stoner criterion, a high DOS can lead to spontaneous spin polarization and magnetic ordering, which, as observed in our sensitivity analysis, introduces significant structural ambiguity. Alternatively, a high DOS at the Fermi level makes the lattice susceptible to Peierls distortions or Charge Density Wave (CDW) transitions. In these scenarios, the crystal lattice distorts to open an electronic band gap, thereby lowering the overall electronic energy of the system.

Crucially, a lattice that is on the verge of such a structural distortion is inherently "pliant." The energetic barrier to shearing the atomic planes is reduced because the system is already exploring nearby local minima in the potential energy surface. This electronic propensity to distort manifests macroscopically as a reduction in the shear modulus $G_{vrh}$.

Our multi-task classification framework successfully maps this boundary. By integrating `dos_at_fermi`, `en_difference` (capturing bond ionicity), and `M_soc_proxy` (accounting for spin-orbit coupling effects prevalent in heavier metals), the model identifies the precise electronic conditions under which a metastable phase retains its structural rigidity. The framework demonstrates that mechanical robustness in metastable TMDs is not a random occurrence but is strictly governed by the avoidance of high-DOS instabilities and the optimal filling of bonding d-orbitals. The resulting probability map and prioritized candidate list serve as a direct bridge between ab initio electronic structure calculations and targeted experimental synthesis of novel, mechanically robust 2D materials.
