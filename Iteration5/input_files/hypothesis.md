The previous iterations have established that mechanical robustness in TMDs is strongly coupled to electronic density of states (DOS) and d-band filling, yet models consistently struggle with inter-group generalization (e.g., Nb vs. Ni compounds) due to the complex, non-linear influence of Charge Density Wave (CDW) distortions. I hypothesize that the "mechanical viability" of metastable TMDs is not merely a function of static electronic descriptors, but is governed by the **symmetry-breaking potential of the lattice**, specifically the degree of octahedral/prismatic distortion relative to the ideal parent structure. 

Instead of relying on scalar DOS or static d-band filling, I propose a new feature set based on **"Structural Distortion Indices" (SDI)**: the deviation of the actual lattice parameters ($a, b, c, \alpha, \beta, \gamma$) from the ideal high-symmetry prototype (e.g., the ideal $2H$ or $1T$ geometry). By calculating the geometric strain required to transform a distorted metastable phase into its nearest high-symmetry parent, we can quantify the "structural cost" of metastability. I hypothesize that materials with a low structural distortion index but high thermodynamic stability are the true candidates for mechanical robustness, as they represent "locked-in" distorted phases that avoid the softening associated with electronic instabilities. The research plan should involve: 1) Calculating the geometric distance between each metastable TMD and its corresponding high-symmetry prototype; 2) Using this SDI as a primary feature in a Random Forest regressor to predict the elastic anisotropy and shear modulus; and 3) Validating if this geometric descriptor resolves the performance bottleneck observed in Nb-based and CDW-prone compounds, which have historically eluded prediction by purely electronic descriptors.
