The previous iterations have successfully identified that mechanical robustness in metastable TMDs is strongly linked to electronic instabilities (e.g., CDW, Jahn-Teller) and that scalar descriptors like `dos_at_fermi` often introduce noise due to missing data or lack of resolution. I hypothesize that the "mechanical softening" observed in metastable phases is not merely a function of static electronic density, but is specifically driven by the **anisotropy of the electronic bonding network**, which can be better captured by the ratio of the lattice parameters ($c/a$) and the `bond_radius_sum` in interaction with the `d_band_filling`. 

Instead of relying on scalar DOS values, I propose a **Geometric-Electronic Interaction Model**. This model will treat the TMD structure as a network of M-X bonds and evaluate the "geometric frustration" of the lattice—defined as the deviation of the actual `c/a` ratio from the ideal ratio for a given `phase` (polymorph). By calculating a `geometric_strain_index` (the residual of a regression between `c/a` and `volume_per_atom` grouped by `phase`), we can quantify how much a metastable structure is "strained" relative to its ideal geometry. I hypothesize that materials with high `geometric_strain_index` and specific `d_band_filling` values are the primary drivers of mechanical instability. This approach bypasses the missing `dos_at_fermi` data entirely and provides a purely structural/elemental proxy for the electronic instabilities that the previous models struggled to resolve, particularly for the Nb and Mo-based compounds.
