The mechanical robustness of metastable TMDs is governed by the competition between structural lattice energy and the electronic stabilization provided by the specific orbital hybridization of the M-X bond. Previous iterations focused on scalar descriptors like `dos_at_fermi` and `d_band_filling`, which fail to capture the directional nature of covalent bonding in distorted phases (e.g., 1T' or CDW-distorted). I hypothesize that the "mechanical viability" of metastable TMDs can be significantly better predicted by constructing a "Bond-Orbital Anisotropy" (BOA) feature, defined as the ratio of the M-X bond length (from `bond_radius_sum`) to the `c_a_ratio`, weighted by the `en_difference` (ionicity). This feature explicitly captures the geometric distortion of the coordination octahedron/prism. By training a model to predict the residual variance in $G_{vrh}$ that remains unexplained by the current `dos_at_fermi`-based models, we can isolate materials where structural distortion acts as a stabilizing mechanism rather than a softening one. This will allow us to identify "structurally locked" metastable phases that are mechanically robust despite having high DOS at the Fermi level, effectively resolving the current model's inability to generalize across Nb and Mo-based CDW compounds.
