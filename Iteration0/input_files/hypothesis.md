The mechanical robustness of TMDs is not merely a function of electronic density of states, but is fundamentally constrained by the "structural frustration" induced by the mismatch between the metal's d-electron count and the chalcogen's electronegativity, which dictates the propensity for Peierls-like distortions. I hypothesize that the mechanical softening observed in metastable TMDs is driven by the coupling between the `d_band_filling` and the `en_difference`, where specific "islands" of stability exist at integer or half-integer d-band fillings. By constructing a "Structural Frustration Index" (SFI) defined as the interaction term between `d_band_filling` and `en_difference`, and using this as a primary feature in a multi-task learning model that simultaneously predicts `G_vrh` and the presence of `CDW/distorted` phases, we can identify metastable materials that are mechanically robust due to structural locking rather than just electronic stability. This approach moves beyond simple binary classification of stiffness by explicitly modeling the competition between lattice distortion (which lowers energy) and elastic modulus (which resists deformation), allowing us to identify "stiff-distorted" phases that were previously overlooked by models focusing solely on the median shear modulus.
