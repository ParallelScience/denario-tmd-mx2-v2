### 1. Definition of the Mechanical Robustness Boundary

The primary objective of this study is to establish a predictive framework capable of identifying mechanically robust metastable transition-metal dichalcogenides (TMDs) based purely on their electronic and compositional signatures. Metastable materials offer a vast, largely untapped chemical space for discovering novel electronic, magnetic, and catalytic properties. However, their experimental synthesis is frequently hindered by mechanical instabilities; materials that are thermodynamically metastable often suffer from soft phonon modes or low shear resistance, leading to structural collapse during or immediately after synthesis. By mapping the electronic phase space, this research aims to decouple thermodynamic metastability from mechanical fragility, thereby isolating candidates that possess the structural rigidity required for experimental realization.

The foundation of the classification framework relies on establishing a binary threshold for mechanical robustness. Using the subset of 90 TMDs for which full elastic tensors are available in the Materials Project database, the Voigt-Reuss-Hill average shear modulus (<code>G_vrh</code>) was extracted. The shear modulus is a critical indicator of a material's resistance to shape-changing deformations, which is particularly relevant for layered TMDs prone to interlayer sliding and shear-induced structural phase transitions. The median <code>G_vrh</code> across this stable and near-stable population was calculated to be 14.5785 GPa. This value serves as the empirical boundary: materials exhibiting a <code>G_vrh</code> strictly greater than 14.5785 GPa are classified as "mechanically robust" (class 1), while those below are deemed "non-robust" (class 0). This median split ensures a perfectly balanced training dataset comprising exactly 45 robust and 45 non-robust samples, thereby preventing class imbalance biases during model training.

A critical preprocessing step involved the rigorous handling of missing electronic density of states (<code>dos_at_fermi</code>) data to prevent data leakage and maintain physical consistency. Among the dataset, 16 materials lacked computed DOS values. For the 6 semiconducting materials—defined by a density functional theory (DFT) band gap strictly greater than 0.1 eV—the <code>dos_at_fermi</code> was explicitly imputed as 0.0 states/eV, accurately reflecting their gapped electronic structure. For the remaining 10 metallic samples (band gap <= 0.1 eV), a K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) regressor was deployed. Utilizing <code>band_gap</code>, <code>d_band_filling</code>, <code>en_difference</code>, and <code>M_soc_proxy</code> as predictive features, the KNN model yielded a mean imputed DOS of 3.5271 states/eV with a standard deviation of 1.9647. Crucially, to ensure the integrity of the cross-validation process, this KNN imputation was dynamically fit exclusively on the training data within each fold, thereby preventing any forward-looking bias in the model's performance metrics.

### 2. Model Generalization and Sensitivity to Structural Density

To rigorously evaluate the model's capacity to generalize across distinct chemical spaces, a Leave-One-Metal-Out (LOMO) cross-validation strategy was implemented across the 13 unique transition metals present in the dataset. By iteratively holding out all compounds containing a specific transition metal, the Random Forest classifier was forced to learn fundamental, universally applicable electronic and compositional rules rather than memorizing metal-specific artifacts.

The base model, utilizing 19 features (explicitly excluding volume and energy above the convex hull), achieved a global pooled Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve (AUPRC) of 0.5997. Performance varied significantly across the held-out metals, reflecting the complex chemical diversity of the TMD family. The model demonstrated excellent predictive capability for Chromium, Manganese, Tantalum, and Vanadium-based compounds, achieving AUPRC scores ranging from 0.8520 to 1.0000. This indicates that the electronic rules learned from the rest of the periodic table successfully generalized to these metals. Conversely, performance was notably lower for Niobium-based compounds (AUPRC = 0.3111), suggesting that Nb-TMDs possess unique mechanical-electronic relationships—perhaps driven by specific charge density wave (CDW) instabilities—that are not fully captured by the global trends of other transition metals. Folds with single-class test sets (Iron, Hafnium, Nickel, Zirconium) yielded undefined individual AUPRCs but contributed accurately to the global pooled metric.

A pivotal component of the analytical pipeline was the sensitivity analysis regarding structural density. When <code>volume_per_atom</code> was introduced into the feature set, the global pooled AUPRC increased substantially from 0.5997 to 0.7548, representing a significant information gain of 0.1551. This quantitative leap underscores a well-established materials science principle: atomic packing density is a dominant, first-order physical determinant of elastic stiffness. However, the explicit exclusion of volume in the primary predictive model represents a deliberate and strategic design choice. For metastable materials, DFT-relaxed volumes can be highly sensitive to the choice of exchange-correlation functional and may not accurately reflect the true synthesized room-temperature structure due to thermal expansion, zero-point energy effects, or defect-induced lattice swelling. By demonstrating that a purely electronic and compositional feature space can still achieve a robust predictive AUPRC of ~0.60, the results validate the hypothesis that mechanical robustness is deeply encoded in the electronic structure. This allows for the effective and reliable screening of metastable phases even when precise volumetric data is unavailable or highly uncertain.

### 3. Electronic and Structural Drivers of Mechanical Stiffness

To unravel the physical mechanisms driving the model's predictions, an analysis of the Permutation Feature Importance was conducted across the LOMO folds. The top three features dictating mechanical robustness were the chalcogen atomic radius (<code>X_atomic_radius</code>, mean AUPRC decrease: 0.0385 +/- 0.0615), the ordinal-encoded crystal system (<code>crystal_system_ord</code>, 0.0300 +/- 0.0581), and the 2H-like phase classification (<code>phase_2H-like</code>, 0.0227 +/- 0.0510).

The prominence of <code>X_atomic_radius</code> underscores the critical role of the chalcogen anion in mediating interlayer interactions and overall lattice polarizability. Larger chalcogens (Tellurium > Selenium > Sulfur) increase the interlayer van der Waals gap and possess more diffuse, highly polarizable electron clouds. This generally leads to a softer lattice and reduced resistance to shear forces. Consequently, the model heavily relies on the chalcogen identity to establish a baseline for mechanical stiffness. Furthermore, the high importance of <code>crystal_system_ord</code> and <code>phase_2H-like</code> highlights the profound impact of structural symmetry. The 2H and 2H-like phases, characterized by trigonal prismatic coordination, often exhibit distinct elastic tensor symmetries compared to the octahedral 1T phases or distorted monoclinic structures. The model leverages these symmetry proxies to differentiate between inherently stiff and compliant structural motifs.

While their direct permutation importance scores were lower, the physical significance of <code>d_band_filling</code>, <code>M_soc_proxy</code>, and <code>dos_at_fermi</code> is central to the electronic phase-space mapping of TMDs. In transition metal dichalcogenides, the fractional filling of the d-band dictates the position of the Fermi level within the crystal field-split d-orbital manifold (e.g., d_z^2, d_xy, d_x^2-y^2). Materials with specific d-band fillings that place the Fermi level at a peak in the density of states (high <code>dos_at_fermi</code>) are highly susceptible to electronic instabilities. According to the Jahn-Teller theorem, non-linear crystals with a degenerate ground state will undergo a geometrical distortion that removes the degeneracy, thereby lowering the overall energy of the system. In the solid state, this manifests as Peierls transitions or charge density wave formations. These symmetry-breaking distortions are intrinsically accompanied by a softening of the acoustic phonon modes, which macroscopically translates to a reduced shear modulus and compromised mechanical stability.

Conversely, spin-orbit coupling (parameterized by <code>M_soc_proxy</code>, the square of the atomic number Z^2) acts as a competing energy scale. Strong SOC can lift orbital degeneracies and split the d-bands without requiring a structural distortion. By effectively reducing the density of states at the Fermi level, strong SOC suppresses the thermodynamic driving force for Jahn-Teller-like distortions. Consequently, the lattice retains its high-symmetry configuration and its mechanical stiffness. The machine learning model implicitly captures this complex interplay: high d-band filling combined with low SOC and high DOS serves as an electronic signature of mechanical softening, whereas configurations that avoid these electronic bottlenecks are predicted to maintain a rigid, robust lattice.

### 4. Identification and Prioritization of Metastable Candidates

Having established and interpreted the classification boundary, the final Random Forest model (optimized with <code>n_estimators</code>=50, <code>max_depth</code>=3, <code>min_samples_split</code>=10) was deployed to screen the 112 metastable TMD candidates lacking computed elastic constants.

To ensure that the predicted "robust" materials are not prone to extreme, unphysical lattice distortions, a secondary structural soundness filter based on the universal elastic anisotropy index was applied. Using a Random Forest regressor trained on the 90-sample elastic subset, the elastic anisotropy for the candidates was predicted. Materials exceeding the 90th-percentile threshold of the training set (104.0408) were deemed structurally precarious and highly susceptible to microcracking or spontaneous deformation. This filter successfully removed 17 highly anisotropic candidates, leaving 95 mechanically viable metastable structures.

From this filtered pool, candidates exhibiting a high predicted probability of robustness (<code>P(robust) > 0.75</code>) were prioritized. This rigorous screening yielded 24 high-priority metastable TMDs. The top echelon of these candidates is heavily dominated by late transition metals, specifically Nickel, Cobalt, and Iron paired with Sulfur and Selenium. The highest-ranked candidates include various polymorphs of NiS_2 (e.g., mp-849053 with <code>P=0.9126</code>; mp-849059 with <code>P=0.9088</code>), NiSe_2 (mp-1071078, <code>P=0.8924</code>), CoS_2 (mp-1042106, <code>P=0.8352</code>), and FeSe_2 (mp-1103177, <code>P=0.8241</code>).

The distribution of these candidates in the electronic phase space is visualized through a scatter plot mapping the predicted probability of robustness against the standardized d-band filling. The 112 metastable candidates are distributed across the three chalcogens (71 sulfides, 23 selenides, and 18 tellurides). The visualization reveals a distinct clustering phenomenon: materials with higher d-band fillings—characteristic of late transition metals such as Iron, Cobalt, and Nickel—consistently achieve higher predicted probabilities of mechanical robustness. This trend suggests that within the metastable regime, the tightly bound, highly populated d-orbitals of late transition metals contribute to a stiffer lattice framework. This enhanced rigidity may arise from increased covalency in the metal-chalcogen bonds or the specific avoidance of soft-phonon modes that typically plague the metastable phases of early transition metals.

### 5. Comparative Analysis of Shear Moduli

To quantitatively validate the physical plausibility of the high-priority candidates, their predicted shear moduli were compared against the actual shear moduli of the known, thermodynamically stable TMD population. A Random Forest regressor was trained on the 90-sample elastic subset to predict the continuous <code>G_vrh</code> values for the 24 high-priority candidates.

The resulting histogram comparison reveals a striking divergence between the two populations. The 35 thermodynamically stable TMDs exhibit a broad distribution of shear moduli, characterized by a mean <code>G_vrh</code> of 26.5473 GPa and a substantial standard deviation of 26.6538 GPa. This wide variance reflects the diverse structural nature of stable TMDs, which encompasses both ultra-soft, easily cleavable layered materials (such as the standard 2H phase of MoS_2) and stiffer, more densely packed 3D-networked phases.

In stark contrast, the 24 high-priority metastable candidates possess a significantly shifted and narrower distribution, with a mean predicted <code>G_vrh</code> of 44.8144 GPa and a standard deviation of 14.8033 GPa. This statistical divergence indicates that the electronic phase-space mapping has successfully isolated a specific sub-population of metastable materials that are, on average, nearly 70% stiffer than the typical stable TMD. The fact that these metastable phases exhibit such high predicted shear moduli implies that their thermodynamic metastability (quantified by their energy above the convex hull) is not a consequence of mechanical fragility. Instead, they likely reside in deep, rigid local minima on the potential energy surface, making them highly resistant to shear-induced phase transformations.

### 6. Conclusion and Implications for Experimental Synthesis

The identification of these 24 mechanically robust metastable TMDs carries profound implications for experimental materials science. A major bottleneck in the synthesis of metastable phases—often achieved via non-equilibrium techniques such as chemical vapor deposition, molecular beam epitaxy, or high-pressure high-temperature (HPHT) synthesis—is the risk of structural collapse or amorphization upon removal of the stabilizing stimulus (e.g., releasing pressure or cooling to room temperature).

By prioritizing candidates with exceptionally high predicted shear moduli and low elastic anisotropy, this risk is drastically reduced. Materials like the highly ranked NiS_2, CoS_2, and FeSe_2 polymorphs are predicted to possess the mechanical rigidity necessary to maintain their metastable crystalline integrity under ambient conditions. Furthermore, the reliance of the predictive model on fundamental electronic descriptors (<code>d_band_filling</code>, <code>M_soc_proxy</code>, <code>dos_at_fermi</code>) rather than purely volumetric data ensures that these predictions are robust against the structural uncertainties inherent in metastable DFT calculations.

Ultimately, this electronic phase-space mapping provides a targeted, high-confidence roadmap for experimentalists. By focusing synthesis efforts on these 24 mechanically viable candidates, researchers can accelerate the discovery of novel TMD phases with unique electronic and magnetic properties, secure in the knowledge that the underlying lattice possesses the mechanical robustness required for practical integration into next-generation devices.
