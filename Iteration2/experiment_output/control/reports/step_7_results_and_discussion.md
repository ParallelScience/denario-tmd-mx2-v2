## Results and Discussion

### Data Preprocessing and Dataset Shift Analysis
The integrity of any machine learning framework applied to materials informatics relies heavily on the quality and representativeness of the underlying dataset. In this study, the initial dataset comprised 202 binary MX$_2$ transition-metal dichalcogenides (TMDs). A critical electronic descriptor, the density of states at the Fermi level (`dos_at_fermi`), was missing for 16 materials due to the absence of corresponding calculations in the Materials Project database. To retain these materials without introducing significant bias, we employed a K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) imputation strategy based on a robustly scaled feature space encompassing structural, thermodynamic, and elemental properties. The imputation successfully estimated the missing values, and we quantified the confidence of this process by calculating the standard deviation of the `dos_at_fermi` among the nearest neighbors. This newly engineered feature, `imputation_uncertainty`, exhibited a range from 0.0 to 4.056 states/eV. By explicitly tracking this uncertainty, we ensured that downstream candidate prioritization could penalize or filter out materials with highly uncertain electronic profiles.

A more systemic challenge in materials property prediction is the presence of dataset shift, particularly when target properties (such as elastic constants) are only available for a biased subset of the data. In our dataset, the Voigt-Reuss-Hill shear modulus ($G_{vrh}$) was only computed for 90 out of the 202 materials, predominantly those that are thermodynamically stable or near-stable. To rigorously assess whether this 90-sample subset was representative of the broader chemical space—specifically the 112 metastable materials lacking elastic data—we conducted two-sample Kolmogorov-Smirnov (KS) tests. We focused on two fundamental descriptors: the fractional filling of the d-band (`d_band_filling`) and the Pauling electronegativity of the transition metal (`M_en`). The KS test for `d_band_filling` yielded a test statistic of 0.1359 with a corresponding $p$-value of 0.2815. Similarly, the test for `M_en` resulted in a statistic of 0.1562 and a $p$-value of 0.1536. Because both $p$-values are well above the standard statistical significance threshold of $\alpha = 0.05$, we fail to reject the null hypothesis that the two samples are drawn from the same continuous distribution. This is a crucial finding: it demonstrates that despite the selective computation of elastic properties by the Materials Project, the available 90-sample subset does not suffer from a severe distributional shift in key electronic and elemental dimensions. Consequently, models trained on this subset can be generalized to the metastable population with a higher degree of confidence.

### Model Performance and the Role of Electronic Descriptors
The central objective of this research was to formulate a binary classification framework capable of identifying mechanically robust metastable TMDs. Rather than attempting to regress continuous elastic constants—a task often plagued by high variance when extrapolating to metastable regimes—we framed the problem as identifying materials that exceed a specific mechanical threshold. We defined the target variable, `is_robust`, based on the 70th percentile of the shear modulus ($G_{vrh}$) computed exclusively within the thermodynamically stable population. This rigorous definition established a robustness threshold of 20.44 GPa. When applied to the 90-sample training subset, this threshold yielded a highly imbalanced class distribution: 20 materials (22.22%) were classified as robust (True), while 70 materials (77.78%) were classified as non-robust (False). This imbalance accurately reflects the physical reality that high shear resistance is a relatively rare trait among diverse TMD polymorphs.

To capture the complex mapping between material descriptors and mechanical robustness, we deployed a Random Forest ensemble architecture. To ensure robust generalization and prevent data leakage across chemically similar compounds, we implemented a Leave-One-Cluster-Out (LOCO) cross-validation strategy. The transition metals were clustered based on their periodic table group (`M_group`), resulting in 5 stratified LOCO folds. This approach guarantees that the model is evaluated on its ability to predict the properties of materials containing transition metals it has never encountered during training, simulating the exact scenario of discovering novel compounds.

We trained and compared two distinct models within this LOCO framework. The baseline model utilized only static, easily accessible descriptors: the covalent atomic radius of the chalcogen (`X_atomic_radius`), the target-encoded crystal system (`crystal_system_encoded`), and the transition metal group (`M_group`). The full model augmented these static features with a suite of dynamic electronic descriptors, including `d_band_filling`, `dos_at_fermi`, the spin-orbit coupling proxy (`M_soc_proxy`), and the electronegativity difference (`en_difference`). 

The performance differential between the two models was substantial. Evaluated using the Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve (AUPRC)—a metric particularly suited for imbalanced classification tasks—the baseline model achieved a mean AUPRC of 0.6681 across the 5 folds. In contrast, the full model achieved a mean AUPRC of 0.7397. This marked improvement quantitatively validates our core hypothesis: static structural and elemental features are insufficient to fully capture the mechanical stability of TMDs. The inclusion of electronic phase-space dynamics, particularly those relating to orbital filling and Fermi level states, provides critical information that significantly enhances predictive precision. The comparative performance of these models is visually detailed in the precision-recall curve plot, saved as <code>step_3_pr_curve_comparison_1_1776197117.png</code>, which clearly illustrates the full model's superior ability to maintain high precision at higher recall levels.

### Feature Importance and the Physical Stability Boundary
To move beyond black-box predictions and extract physical insights from the full Random Forest model, we employed SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP). SHAP values provide a unified measure of feature importance by quantifying the marginal contribution of each feature to the model's predictions. The SHAP summary bar plot, saved as <code>step_5_shap_summary_bar_1_1776197283.png</code>, delineates the top five most influential features driving the classification of mechanical robustness. 

The transition metal group (`M_group`) emerged as the dominant feature, exhibiting a mean absolute SHAP value of 0.1425. This aligns with established metallurgical intuition, as the group number dictates the valence electron count and fundamentally shapes the bonding character of the metal-chalcogen network. The second most important feature was `d_band_filling` (0.1102), followed by `crystal_system_encoded` (0.0725), `M_soc_proxy` (0.0536), and `en_difference` (0.0392). The high ranking of `d_band_filling` and `en_difference` is particularly illuminating. It suggests that mechanical robustness is not merely a function of atomic packing, but is deeply tied to the electronic structure—specifically, how the d-orbitals are populated and the degree of ionicity in the M-X bonds.

To visualize the complex, non-linear stability boundary defined by these electronic features, we generated a 2D Partial Dependence Plot (serving as an Accumulated Local Effects proxy) mapping the interaction between `d_band_filling` and `en_difference`. This plot, saved as <code>step_5_ale_2d_plot_1_1776197283.png</code>, reveals distinct regions in the electronic phase space associated with high probabilities of mechanical robustness. By overlaying the scatter points of the 46 thermodynamically stable materials onto this 2D landscape, we observed a strong alignment between the model's predicted high-probability zones and the known islands of thermodynamic stability. This convergence suggests that the electronic conditions required to prevent shear-induced mechanical failure are closely related to the conditions required for thermodynamic ground-state stability. The model has effectively learned a physical boundary where optimal d-band filling and bond ionicity synergize to stiffen the crystal lattice against deformation.

### Mechanical Robustness, Spin-Orbit Coupling, and Out-of-Distribution Analysis
A secondary hypothesis of this study was that spin-orbit coupling (SOC) might play a significant role in mechanical softening. In high-density-of-states phases, strong SOC can induce electronic instabilities that couple to the lattice, leading to structural distortions (analogous to Jahn-Teller effects) that compromise the shear modulus. To investigate this, we isolated the 35 stable materials in the training set and calculated the Spearman rank correlation between the SOC proxy (`M_soc_proxy`, defined as $Z^2$ of the metal) and the shear modulus ($G_{vrh}$). 

The statistical analysis yielded a weak negative correlation coefficient of $R = -0.1542$, with a corresponding $p$-value of 0.3763. Because the $p$-value exceeds the standard significance threshold of $\alpha = 0.05$, we must conclude that there is no statistically significant monotonic linear relationship between the SOC proxy and the shear modulus within the stable population. This finding is visually corroborated by the scatter plot saved as <code>step_4_scatter_msoc_gvrh_1_1776197199.png</code>, which shows a highly dispersed distribution without a clear trend. However, it is crucial to reconcile this lack of linear correlation with the fact that `M_soc_proxy` was identified as the fourth most important feature in the non-linear Random Forest model (mean absolute SHAP = 0.0536). This discrepancy implies that the influence of spin-orbit coupling on mechanical robustness is highly non-linear and likely conditional on other factors, such as specific crystal symmetries or magnetic orderings, which the tree-based ensemble is capable of capturing.

When deploying machine learning models to screen metastable materials, the risk of extrapolating into unknown chemical spaces is a primary concern. To quantify and mitigate this risk, we conducted an Out-of-Distribution (OOD) analysis using the Mahalanobis distance metric. We first computed the centroid and the inverse covariance matrix of the 35 stable training samples across the 11-dimensional feature space utilized by the full model. The covariance matrix exhibited a rank of 9. Using a 95% Chi-square distribution threshold corresponding to this rank, we established a strict OOD cutoff distance of 4.1133. 

We then calculated the Mahalanobis distance for each of the 112 metastable candidates. The analysis revealed that 52 candidates, representing 46.43% of the metastable pool, exhibited distances exceeding the threshold and were consequently flagged as OOD. The distribution of these distances, clearly demarcating the in-distribution and out-of-distribution populations, is illustrated in the histogram saved as <code>step_4_hist_mahalanobis_1_1776197199.png</code>. The identification of such a large proportion of OOD candidates underscores the necessity of this step; predictions made on these materials carry a high degree of epistemic uncertainty and should be treated with extreme caution in experimental planning.

### Prioritization of Metastable Candidates for Experimental Synthesis
The culmination of this computational framework is the generation of a highly refined, prioritized list of metastable TMD candidates recommended for experimental synthesis. The initial pool of 112 metastable materials was subjected to a rigorous, multi-tiered filtering process designed to isolate only those candidates that exhibit high predicted mechanical robustness, structural stability, and high model confidence.

The filtering criteria were defined as follows:
1. **High Probability of Robustness**: Candidates must possess a predicted probability of being mechanically robust (`prob_is_robust`) greater than 0.75, as determined by the full Random Forest classifier.
2. **Structural Stability**: Candidates must exhibit a predicted universal elastic anisotropy index (`pred_elastic_anisotropy`) below 135.82, which corresponds to the 90th percentile of the predicted values. High anisotropy is often a precursor to structural instability and delamination in layered materials.
3. **In-Distribution Confidence**: Candidates must not be flagged as Out-of-Distribution (`is_ood` == False), ensuring they reside within the Mahalanobis distance threshold of 4.1133 relative to the stable training population.
4. **Data Quality**: Candidates must have an imputation uncertainty (`imputation_uncertainty`) less than or equal to 1.0 states/eV, ensuring that the underlying electronic descriptors are reliable.

Applying these stringent filters drastically reduced the candidate pool from 112 to a highly exclusive list of 16 materials. This aggressive reduction highlights the selectivity of the framework and provides experimentalists with a highly targeted, low-risk portfolio of materials to pursue.

The prioritized list is heavily dominated by late transition metals, specifically nickel, iron, and cobalt-based dichalcogenides. The highest-ranked candidates are various polymorphs of nickel disulfide (NiS$_2$) and nickel diselenide (NiSe$_2$). The top overall candidate is a CDW/distorted phase of NiSe$_2$ (mp-1071078), which achieved an exceptional robustness probability of 0.9642 and a predicted elastic anisotropy of -8.9346. Crucially, its Mahalanobis distance of 2.7962 places it comfortably within the in-distribution regime, and it required no DOS imputation (`imputation_uncertainty` = 0.0).

Following closely are several phases of NiS$_2$, including a CDW/distorted phase (mp-849059, `prob_is_robust` = 0.9620, distance = 3.6611), a 2H phase (mp-849079, `prob_is_robust` = 0.9607, distance = 2.8738), and a cubic phase (mp-849053, `prob_is_robust` = 0.9581, distance = 2.8478). The prominence of CDW/distorted and 2H phases in these high-ranking nickel compounds suggests that specific lattice distortions, potentially driven by charge density waves, may actually lock the lattice into mechanically rigid configurations, preventing shear failure.

Beyond nickel, the framework also identified highly promising iron and cobalt candidates. The 3R phase of FeS$_2$ (mp-1430160) emerged with a robustness probability of 0.9043 and a predicted elastic anisotropy of -81.8684, alongside an in-distribution Mahalanobis distance of 3.1109. Similarly, the 3R phase of CoS$_2$ (mp-1387852) achieved a probability of 0.9034 and a distance of 3.4574. The identification of the 3R (rhombohedral) phase for both FeS$_2$ and CoS$_2$ is a significant finding, indicating that this specific stacking sequence may offer superior mechanical resilience compared to the more common 1T or 2H polymorphs for these specific metals.

Other notable candidates that passed all filters include a 1T phase of FeTe$_2$ (mp-1224800, `prob_is_robust` = 0.8543) and a 1T phase of CoTe$_2$ (mp-1009641, `prob_is_robust` = 0.8176). The inclusion of tellurides in the final list demonstrates the model's ability to identify robust candidates across the entire chalcogen series, provided the electronic and structural conditions are met.

In conclusion, the integration of electronic phase-space mapping, rigorous cross-validation, and strict out-of-distribution filtering has yielded a robust predictive framework for metastable TMDs. The final prioritized list of 16 candidates represents a highly curated selection of materials that exhibit the electronic signatures of mechanical robustness while remaining within the reliable predictive domain of the model. These findings provide a clear, data-driven roadmap for the experimental synthesis of novel, mechanically viable transition-metal dichalcogenides, significantly accelerating the materials discovery pipeline by focusing resources on the most promising regions of the chemical phase space.
